Building a product roadmap
for a $5M business
with no PM playbook.

How I translated customer signal from JCK Expo and behavioral data into a roadmap and GTM strategy that drove a 30% lift in jewellery design performance — while acting as the sole bridge between customers, design, and production.

Timeline3 years (Feb 2023 – Jan 2026)
RoleProduct Marketing Specialist
CompanyCostar Imports · San Jose, CA
Outcome30% design performance lift

Context

Costar Imports is a B2B jewellery importer serving retailers and boutiques across the US, with a customer portfolio totalling $5M in revenue. When I joined, there was no formalized product roadmap, no structured customer research process, and no systematic way to translate what customers said at trade shows into decisions the production factory could act on.

I was hired as a product marketing specialist. I ended up functioning as a de facto PM. The role required me to build the infrastructure for customer insight — and then use it.

"The riskiest assumption in B2B product work is that the person who places the order and the person who wears the product want the same thing. At Costar, they almost never did."

The process

01

Customer discovery at scale — JCK Expo

America's largest jewellery trade show gave me direct access to hundreds of retail buyers in a compressed window. I built a structured listening approach: not just what customers said they wanted, but what they stopped at, what they picked up, what they put down, and what questions they asked. I came back with pattern-level insight, not just quotes.

02

Translating behavioral data into roadmap inputs

I paired the qualitative expo signal with quantitative behavioral data — purchase patterns, return rates by design category, repeat order behavior by segment. The combination revealed which design categories had latent demand versus which ones were simply being ordered out of habit. That gap became the roadmap's first priority.

03

Building the internal product roadmap

Working with the dedicated sales representative, I built the first structured internal roadmap: prioritized design categories, market trend inputs, and customer segment needs mapped to a rolling timeline. The format was simple by design — the audience was production and sales, not engineers, so the artifact had to be actionable without being technical.

04

GTM strategy and product narratives

Each design category needed a narrative: not a product description, but a story that a retail buyer could tell their own customer. I developed product documentation and go-to-market materials that framed the collection in terms of the end customer's desire, not the importer's inventory. This is the same principle as positioning in tech GTM — you sell the job to be done, not the feature.

05

Closing the feedback loop with production

The final leg of the loop was translating customer feedback and design KPIs back to the factory. I acted as the bridge — taking what I'd learned from retail buyers and converting it into design briefs the production team could actually act on. This required understanding both sides of the conversation: what "customers want more organic shapes" means to a retail buyer and what it means to someone cutting metal in a factory.

What I learned

  • In B2B, your customer's customer is the real user. I spent three years learning to hold both perspectives simultaneously: what the retail buyer needed commercially, and what the end consumer needed emotionally. Most product decisions at Costar lived in the gap between those two.
  • A roadmap without a narrative is just a list. The hardest part of this work wasn't the research — it was turning findings into a prioritization argument that people with different incentives (sales, production, ownership) could all read as obviously correct.
  • Trade shows are user research, not just sales. JCK taught me how to run structured observation at scale: watch what people do, not just what they say. I brought that same instinct from my newsroom days — reading a room, reading a crowd, extracting signal from behavior.
  • Owning the full loop changes the quality of the input. Because I was involved from research to factory feedback, I had unusually high fidelity signal at every stage. The 30% lift wasn't one decision — it was the compound effect of tighter feedback loops across the whole system.
